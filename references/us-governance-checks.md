# US Governance Checks for Small and Mid-Cap Equities

Use this file when reviewing SEC filings, insider behavior, shareholder structure, board incentives, SPAC history, and related-party risk. US disclosure is better than many markets, but better disclosure does not mean better treatment of common shareholders. Small-cap governance abuse usually hides in legal financing terms, compensation, related-party contracts, weak boards, and timing.

---

## 1. SEC Filing Checklist

Start with EDGAR. Pull the filing history before reading the story.

Required filings to check:

| Filing | What to extract | Why it matters |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| 10-K | Business, risk factors, MD&A, notes, debt, revenue recognition, controls | Annual baseline and full risk disclosure |
| 10-Q | Recent liquidity, burn, debt changes, customer concentration | Detect deterioration before annual report |
| 8-K | Financings, customer deals, management changes, guidance, compliance notices | Small-cap facts often arrive here first |
| DEF 14A | ownership, pay, board, RPTs, poison pills, voting structure | Governance core document |
| S-1 / S-3 | resale shares, shelves, registration rights, selling holders | Future dilution and overhang |
| S-8 | employee plan share registration | Compensation dilution feedstock |
| 13D / 13G | activist, strategic, or passive large holders | Control pressure and holder quality |
| Form 3 / 4 / 5 | insider transactions | Insider alignment or exit behavior |
| NT 10-K / NT 10-Q | late filing notices | Accounting or control trouble signal |
| 424B / prospectus supplement | ATM, convert, PIPE, warrant details | Real financing terms |

Filing red flags:

- NT 10-K or NT 10-Q followed by auditor change, restatement, or internal control weakness.
- Multiple 8-Ks with financing terms but no plain explanation of total dilution.
- Risk factors expand sharply around liquidity, customer concentration, or going concern.
- Management stops disclosing a metric it used to promote.
- Shelf registration appears soon after a retail-driven price spike.

## 2. Board and Management Alignment

Read DEF 14A and latest 10-K.

Check:

- CEO, CFO, chair roles and tenure.
- Board independence under exchange rules and in substance.
- Audit committee expertise.
- Compensation versus company size, revenue, cash, and burn.
- Performance targets: revenue, adjusted EBITDA, share price, clinical milestones, or vague operational goals.
- Equity awards: strike price, vesting conditions, repricing, reload grants.
- Severance and change-of-control payments.
- Director overboarding and related business ties.

Weak alignment patterns:

- Management owns little common stock but receives large annual option grants.
- Bonuses pay out despite negative shareholder returns, dilution, or missed guidance.
- Adjusted EBITDA targets exclude the costs that keep recurring.
- Board has long tenure, personal ties, or compensation that makes independence theoretical.
- Executives receive retention awards while the company raises dilutive capital.

Score management ownership carefully. High insider ownership can align incentives, but if insiders hold super-voting stock or cheap founder shares, their downside may differ from public holders.

## 3. Insider Trading and Form 4 Patterns

Pull Forms 3, 4, and 5 for officers, directors, and 10% holders.

Classify every transaction:

- Open-market purchase.
- Open-market sale.
- Option exercise and hold.
- Option exercise and sale.
- RSU vesting and tax sale.
- 10b5-1 plan sale.
- Gift, transfer, or trust movement.

Strong signals:

- Multiple officers buy common stock in the open market with personal cash.
- Insider purchases occur after bad news and before no obvious catalyst.
- Executives exercise options and hold the shares.

Weak signals:

- Repeated sales under 10b5-1 plans while management promotes near-term upside.
- 10b5-1 plan adoption shortly before heavy selling.
- Sales cluster before earnings misses, financing, trial failure, guidance cuts, or customer loss.
- Insiders only receive stock, then sell for taxes and never buy.
- Directors buy token amounts that are tiny versus annual compensation.

Rule 10b5-1 checks:

- Adoption date, modification date, termination date.
- Cooling-off period. Modern plans generally require a cooling-off period for officers and directors.
- Whether the plan was adopted while the company likely had material nonpublic information.
- Whether the plan is used as a shield for opportunistic selling.

Do not overstate one Form 4. Pattern matters more than a single sale.

## 4. 13F and Institutional Ownership Analysis

Use 13F data for institutional holders, but understand the limits. 13F reports long US equity positions with a delay. It does not show short positions, many derivatives, or real-time changes.

Check:

- Top 20 institutional holders and percentage of shares.
- Holder quality: long-only funds, index funds, quant funds, hedge funds, activists, venture holders, SPAC sponsors.
- Quarter-over-quarter position changes.
- Concentration among holders likely to sell into liquidity.
- Whether large holders are passive index funds rather than conviction owners.
- New 13D activist filings versus passive 13G filings.

Interpretation:

- Rising institutional ownership can validate a story, but it can also reflect index inclusion.
- High institutional ownership plus high short interest can create crowded positioning and squeeze risk if float is tight.
- Venture or sponsor holders with low cost basis are an overhang after lock-up expiry.
- A famous fund holding 1% of its portfolio in the stock is not the same as a high-conviction bet.

Red flags:

- Institutions exit while retail volume rises.
- Only passive holders remain, with no active fundamental ownership.
- PIPE investors or selling stockholders register large blocks through S-1 or S-3 resale filings.
- 13F shows a holder, but the company uses that name heavily in marketing without noting position size or filing lag.

## 5. Share Class Structures and Voting Control

Review certificate of incorporation, DEF 14A, 10-K, and IPO/SPAC filings.

Check:

- Single class versus dual-class or multi-class stock.
- Votes per share for each class.
- Economic ownership versus voting control.
- Sunset provisions: time-based, ownership threshold, founder death, transfer.
- Conversion rights.
- Protective provisions for preferred stock.
- Whether insiders can control board elections, mergers, compensation plans, or charter amendments.

Red flags:

- Insiders control voting with less than 20% economic ownership.
- No sunset on super-voting shares.
- Public holders have little say over directors or major corporate actions.
- Preferred holders have consent rights that can block common shareholder outcomes.
- Founder control persists after operating performance collapses.

Dual-class is not automatically fatal. It becomes a governance problem when paired with poor disclosure, heavy compensation, dilution, or related-party transactions.

## 6. Poison Pills and Anti-Takeover Defenses

Check DEF 14A, 8-Ks, charter, bylaws, and shareholder rights plan filings.

Common defenses:

- Poison pill or shareholder rights plan.
- Classified board.
- Blank-check preferred stock.
- Supermajority vote requirements.
- Limits on calling special meetings.
- Written consent restrictions.
- Advance notice bylaws.
- Exclusive forum provisions.

Interpretation:

- A short-term pill after a hostile approach may protect value.
- A long-term pill at a chronically underperforming small-cap often protects management jobs.
- A poison pill paired with low insider ownership and high compensation is a red flag.

Questions to answer:

- What ownership threshold triggers the pill?
- When does it expire?
- Was it adopted after activist buying or after the share price fell?
- Does it block a rational sale process?

## 7. SPAC Origin Checks

If the company came public through a SPAC, read the S-4/proxy, 8-K closing docs, PIPE subscription agreements, and resale registration statements.

Extract:

- Original SPAC sponsor and promote size.
- Redemption rate at de-SPAC.
- PIPE investors, price, discounts, warrants, registration rights.
- Earnout shares and trigger prices.
- Lock-up expiry dates.
- Public warrants and private placement warrants.
- Projections used in the merger proxy versus actual results.
- Related-party ties between sponsor, target, PIPE investors, and board.

Red flags:

- Redemption rate above 70%, leaving the company undercapitalized.
- Sponsor promote remains large while public shareholders funded little cash.
- Original projections missed by more than 30% within two years.
- Earnout shares create dilution at prices close to current trading levels.
- PIPE investors received better terms than public holders.
- Frequent management turnover after de-SPAC.

SPAC math is usually ugly. Rebuild the cap table from the closing 8-K and later S-1 resale filings. Do not trust a current share count from a quote page if warrants, earnouts, converts, or preferred stock exist.

## 8. Related-Party Detection in the US Context

US related-party abuse is often legal and disclosed in dull language. Read the related person transactions section in DEF 14A and footnotes in 10-K/10-Q.

Common patterns:

- Office, lab, data center, or mining site leased from insiders or entities tied to insiders.
- Consulting agreements with directors, founders, family members, or sponsor affiliates.
- Loans from officers, directors, sponsor entities, or major shareholders.
- Asset purchases from insiders or affiliates.
- Customer or supplier relationships with entities controlled by insiders.
- Family members employed in paid roles.
- Shared services agreements with sponsor or parent entities.
- Royalty agreements or IP licenses with founder-controlled entities.

Checks:

- Dollar amount versus revenue, cash, SG&A, and market cap.
- Pricing terms versus market rates.
- Approval process: audit committee, independent directors, fairness opinion.
- Duration and termination rights.
- Whether the related party has priority over common shareholders in a downside case.

Red flags:

- Related-party payments rise while revenue falls.
- Terms are described as “market” without evidence.
- Same related party appears as lender, landlord, customer, and shareholder.
- Company buys assets from insiders shortly before impairment.
- Sponsor affiliates receive fees after a SPAC deal underperforms.

## 9. Governance Verdict Scoring

Count red flags. Do not bury them in prose.

| Red Flags | Governance View | Valuation Treatment |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------------|
| 0 | Acceptable | Normal sector multiple possible |
| 1-2 | Watch | Apply discount or demand catalyst |
| 3-4 | High risk | Value trap risk, require large discount and clean catalyst |
| 5+ | Uninvestable governance | Common equity likely deserves no long-term capital |

Governance can override valuation. A cheap stock with management using public shareholders as financing inventory is not cheap. It is inventory.
